Core Concepts at a Glance

Preferred stock is defined by a few key features that give it a "preferred" status over common stock.

Fixed Dividends

Preferred stocks typically pay a fixed, regular dividend, much like a bond's interest payment. This provides a predictable income stream, which is a primary attraction for income-focused investors.

Liquidation Priority

If a company goes bankrupt, preferred shareholders get paid before common shareholders. They stand in line after bondholders but have a senior claim on assets over common equity.

No Voting Rights

This priority comes at a cost: preferred stockholders usually don't get to vote on corporate matters. Control is retained by common shareholders, making preferred stock a way to raise capital without diluting control.

Where Does It Fit?

The true nature of preferred stock is best understood by comparing it to its neighbors in the capital structure: common stock and bonds. It's a hybrid, borrowing traits from both.

Bonds (Debt)

Lowest risk, fixed interest payments, highest priority in liquidation. No ownership or growth potential.

Preferred Stock (Hybrid)

Medium risk, fixed dividends, middle priority. Limited growth potential but higher yields than bonds.

Common Stock (Equity)

Highest risk, variable dividends (if any), lowest priority. Highest potential for growth.

A Spectrum of Styles

Not all preferred stocks are created equal. Issuers can customize them with different features to attract different investors. Click through the tabs to explore the most common varieties found in the U.S. market.

Cumulative Preferred Stock

Offers a significant safety net. If the company skips a dividend payment, the unpaid amount accumulates. All these "dividends in arrears" must be paid to cumulative preferred shareholders before any dividends can be paid to common shareholders.

Callable (Redeemable) Preferred Stock

Gives the issuing company the right to buy back the shares at a predetermined price after a certain date. Companies often do this if interest rates fall, allowing them to refinance at a lower dividend rate. This introduces "call risk" for the investor, capping their upside.

Convertible Preferred Stock

Allows the holder to convert their preferred shares into a fixed number of common shares. This feature offers the safety of fixed dividends with the potential for capital appreciation if the company's common stock performs well.

Participating Preferred Stock

Provides a base fixed dividend plus an additional dividend if the company hits certain profit targets. In a liquidation, holders get their initial investment back first, and then also "participate" with common stockholders in the remaining assets.
